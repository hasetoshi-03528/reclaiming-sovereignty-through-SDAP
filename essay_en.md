# Sanctum Build — Reclaiming Sovereignty through S.D.A.P.

## Prologue: The Critical Point of Computing Civilization

More than a quarter century into the twenty-first century, we find ourselves standing in the midst of a strange paradox. The performance of computers has improved exponentially, and artificial intelligence is beginning to replace much of what we once called human intellectual work. And yet, the intelligence of the humans who *use* these machines appears to be moving in the opposite direction. The more sophisticated the tools we acquire, the less we understand their inner workings — is this what we should call progress, or does it demand a different name entirely?

This essay takes that question as its point of departure and examines the problem of the loss of "technological sovereignty" in the contemporary world. In response, it proposes S.D.A.P. (Self-Designed with AI-assisted Protocol) — a philosophical and practical protocol for consciously reclaiming that sovereignty. This is not merely a technical argument. It is a philosophical inquiry into the relationship between tools and human beings, the difference between creation and imitation, and the very conditions under which one can be called a *subject*.

To begin, let us consider the phenomenon of the disappearance of the "interface" — what the modern computing environment has taken from us in terms of felt experience.

---

## I. The Disappearance of the "Interface"

In the contemporary world, the computer has become almost entirely transparent. Press the power button, and a polished GUI unfolds within seconds, drawing the user into a frictionless logical plain. The desktop that appears with the startup chime is clean, orderly, and bears no trace of any *resistance*. The user is asked nothing, chooses nothing, and is simply, smoothly connected to a world that already exists.

But there was a time when the dialogue between human and machine was far less smooth. Installing an operating system was, in a sense, a sacred ritual. One drew partition boundaries across the physical terrain of storage, carefully configured the bootloader, and poured in the kernel — the soul of the system. In that process, the operator had no choice but to understand the logical structure of the system. What is a filesystem? Where does a mount point exist? Why is swap space necessary? Those who could not answer these questions could not complete the ritual of installation.

The modern computing environment has quietly, but surely, taken that sense of *resistance* away. Today, a person can "use" a computer perfectly well without knowing how to install an OS. With smartphones, the very concept of "installing an operating system" does not exist. The moment you purchase a device, the system already exists in complete form, and all that is asked of the user is the thoroughly passive act of choosing applications within a given environment.

As this transparency has deepened, most of us have entirely lost the felt sense of the "interface" — that charged zone where hardware and software once clashed and sparked. The sensation of touching the machine's innards directly through a BIOS screen full of austere characters. The tension of a single command line entry immediately actuating physical hardware — the moment when logic and physics directly connected. Such things are becoming esoteric knowledge, known only to specialists and enthusiasts.

The problem is not the loss itself. The truly serious matter is that the loss is not being recognized as a loss. People uncritically accept the environment given to them by manufacturers, wrapping themselves in it like an off-the-rack suit and taking it to be the whole of what a computer is. This lack of literacy is not simply a matter of technical ignorance. It must be read as a symptom of intellectual regression — an attempt to coat over the absolute logical structure of a system with the low-frequency noise of emotional "in-group dynamics" and "the efficiency of imitation."

That human beings are tool-using animals is a recognition so widely shared that it hardly requires Bergson's authority. But between *using* a tool, *understanding* a tool, and *designing* a tool, there are unbridgeable chasms. Computing civilization has succeeded in plastering over these chasms with the adhesive of convenience, rendering their existence invisible. The strange spectacle that results — human beings used by their own tools — is the logical consequence.

---

## II. Anatomy of the "Copy Society" Pathology

The disappearance of the interface does not remain a technical problem. It is deeply entangled with structural problems in society itself.

What the author confronts on a daily basis is the overwhelming sense of *tedium* produced by this intellectual regression. When a router malfunctions, the family abandons any attempt at logical diagnosis and leaps directly to the conclusion that it is "broken." Workplace colleagues consume AI as an "efficiency tool" without critically verifying the validity of its outputs. In meetings, reading the room takes precedence over logical correctness. Underlying all of this is a fundamental absence of respect for engineering as a pursuit of truth.

It would be easy to attribute this to individual laziness. But more precisely, it is a problem of the incentive structure produced by the social system itself. What is rewarded in contemporary organizations is not "understanding deeply" but "processing quickly" — not "designing something original" but "combining existing things cleverly." When this evaluative axis becomes dominant, the act of thinking deeply is eliminated as a cost-inefficient choice.

Ask what the "efficiency" pursued by contemporary society truly amounts to, and the answer is, in the end, nothing more than the mass production of copies. The same code whoever writes it, the same structure whoever designs it, the same conclusion whoever reaches it — when this logic of homogenization governs an organization, a bizarrely inverted ecosystem emerges in which the highest praise goes to whoever can most rapidly produce the correct copy.

Arendt, in *The Human Condition*, classified human activity into three tiers: labor, work, and action. The trap that contemporary knowledge work has fallen into is the reduction of creative and intellectual activity — which properly belongs to the categories of "work" and "action" — into "labor": repetitive, consumed, leaving no trace. This reduction is proceeding everywhere.

In this context, consider again the phenomenon of people with a creator's temperament being compelled to leave organizations. The common interpretation frames this as a lack of cooperation or unmanageability. But that interpretation is superficial. More essentially, the "originality" that creator-type people possess functions as a fatal bug within a system whose supreme directive is homogenization. Instead of mass-producing correct copies, they question the question itself. Instead of following existing frameworks, they try to design the frameworks. This orientation is a foreign body that fundamentally disrupts the operations of the "copy society."

They are excluded not due to a lack of ability, but due to its excess. Within this paradox lies the core of contemporary intellectual regression. When society demands human beings who function as convenient tools, those who design tools are inevitably pushed to the margins. And it is precisely at those margins that genuine thought is born.

Here, one question must be raised. Why can human beings not stop copying?

The answer is simple. Because copying is easy, safe, and even pleasurable. Imitating the correct answer brings immediate approval, and approval triggers dopamine. Once this circuit is formed, the act of thinking independently becomes recorded in the brain as "risk." It might fail. It might be rejected. It might take too long. To avoid that anxiety, human beings retreat into copying.

But there is a fact being overlooked. A brain that repeats copying gradually loses the circuitry for independent thought. It is the same as a muscle. Without use, it atrophies. Before donning the exoskeleton, one must first accept the discomfort of standing on one's own feet. That discomfort is the only load that strengthens the muscle called thought. The pleasure of copying is the entrance to the death of thinking.

## III. AI — A Double-Edged Sword

It was into this era — with the pathology of copy society at its peak — that generative AI arrived.

Generative AI has pushed these problems into a more complex dimension. On one hand, AI can function as a tool that accelerates the "copy-ification" of knowledge work to its ultimate extreme. In an environment where a single prompt generates "plausible text," "plausible code," and "plausible design," the need for human beings to think deeply diminishes ever further. If AI can instantaneously mass-produce the "correct copy," where does the value of human beings doing so reside? Those who cannot answer this question will gradually transform into mere receptors that passively consume AI outputs.

On the other hand, AI is capable of functioning in an entirely different mode. As a dialogue partner for deepening thought, a high-precision sounding board for validating designs, or a critical apparatus that ruthlessly identifies the weaknesses in one's own logic — in this sense, AI can become a powerful auxiliary for expanding human intelligence.

The question is who decides, and how, whether AI operates in which of these modes. Between those who passively consume AI as an "efficiency tool" and those who actively command it as an "intelligence amplifier," there is a difference as vast as heaven and earth in the quality of the experience — even though both are using the same tool. For the former, AI is the master; for the latter, AI is the servant. And this difference is determined not by differences in the tool's capability, but by the presence or absence of "sovereignty" on the part of the human using it.

What is crucial here is the recognition that dependence on AI and utilization of AI are entirely different acts. A carpenter who uses a hammer does not depend on the hammer — the carpenter *wields* it. The key distinction is that the carpenter understands the characteristics of the hammer deeply and can judge for themselves how it should be used in any given situation. The same applies to AI. Only those who understand how AI reasoning works, grasp its limits and possibilities, and can design what questions to ask in what ways to extract maximum utility, can truly "use" AI in any meaningful sense.

One misunderstanding must be cleared up here. S.D.A.P. does not demand the ascetic discipline of "think everything through on your own."

Think for yourself. But use your weapons.

Donning AI as an exoskeleton is not the abandonment of thought — it is the extension of thought. The human brain has physical limits to its computational capacity. Compensating for those limits with the exoskeleton of AI, and concentrating cognitive resources on higher-order questions — this is the correct use of AI within S.D.A.P.

What matters is the sequence. First, stand on your own feet. Develop your own logic. Then don the exoskeleton. The reverse order is not permitted. For one without their own legs, an exoskeleton is not a prosthetic — it is a cage.

There is no need for the despair of "I can't do this." Train the muscle of thought, and the exoskeleton of AI will expand your range exponentially. It is beyond that point that the path opens toward becoming a creator who defines everything — from the physical layer to the ethical layer.

## IV. S.D.A.P. — A Philosophy for Keeping the Self as Subject

With that problem consciousness established, this essay proposes **S.D.A.P. (Self-Designed with AI-assisted Protocol)**.

What is S.D.A.P.? In a single phrase: a philosophical and practical protocol for using AI while continuously maintaining the Self as the active subject. It refuses subordination to AI, yet does not wastefully reject AI. It places a high-order reasoning engine under deliberate control as a "high-precision protocol" for sharpening one's own design philosophy. The essence of this protocol resides in the phrase "Self-Designed." The subject of design must always remain oneself.

The **First Principle** of S.D.A.P. is the **Rejection of Operation Without Understanding**. It demands that before using any tool, one understand the principles of its operation as fully as possible. Before clicking a GUI button, ask what that button triggers. Before sending a prompt to AI, design what that prompt is attempting to elicit. This habit may slow down operations. But it is the speed of one who possesses sovereignty.

The **Second Principle** is **Deliberate Abbreviation After Full Comprehension**. This appears to contradict the First Principle, but is in fact its extension. Take the example of safely ejecting USB storage — the rule says to click the "eject button." An intermediate user blindly obeys this rule. But one who deeply understands filesystem behavior simulates the current mount state, the presence or absence of recent write operations, and the cache policy of the target device — and then, under specific conditions, makes the deliberate choice to simply unplug it. This is not negligence. It is a conscious abbreviation permitted only to those who have fully internalized the logic behind the rule. This "shortcut taken after going all the way around" is the proof that one has reclaimed sovereignty over the system.

The **Third Principle** is the **Maintenance of Asymmetry with AI**. AI must be treated not as an equal partner, but as a highly capable yet subordinate tool. AI output is always a hypothesis, and the judgment to verify, adopt, or reject it belongs to the human. Even if AI produces a "correct answer," if one cannot explain for oneself why it is correct, that answer must not be adopted — this is the strict discipline of S.D.A.P.

How do these three principles function in practice? Consider software development. Having AI generate code is now a routine act. But when an S.D.A.P. practitioner receives AI-generated code, the first act is not "testing" but "reading." What is this code doing? Why was this algorithm chosen? How are edge cases handled? Critically reading the AI's design thinking, and redesigning where necessary — only through that process does AI function as a true "assistant" in any meaningful sense.

Will those who practice S.D.A.P. end up in isolation? Such a question may arise. Does the pursuit of autonomy mean severance from others?

The answer is no. In fact, the opposite is true.

The relationships among those who copy may appear connected on the surface, but in reality they are nothing more than a chain of mirrors reflecting the same correct answer back and forth. No essential exchange takes place there. But when autonomous individuals meet, what occurs is not copying but resonance — synchronization. Logic that each has independently arrived at through their own process of reasoning intersects, whether by chance or by necessity — and in that moment, a spark of new thought is born that cannot be reduced to either logic alone.

What S.D.A.P. aims for is not the isolated genius. It is a higher-order connection in which autonomous individuals resonate with one another. If you seek fellow travelers, the prerequisite is to first become an autonomous source of transmission yourself. Magnets attract magnets.

## V. The Philosophy of "Building" a System

At the philosophical foundation of S.D.A.P. lies a special reverence for the act of "building" a system.

When an architect designs a building, they do not merely envision a beautiful exterior or comfortable interior space. Beginning with the structure of the foundation, they consider the distribution of loads, the properties of materials, and the balance between durability and flexibility — designing the building as a single integrated system. The design of a computer system is, in essence, exactly the same. Grasping as a whole the layers that accumulate behind the interface — the application layer, the OS layer, the driver layer, the hardware layer — and designing the responsibilities and interactions of each layer.

In this act of "building," the designer inevitably confronts fundamental questions. What does this system exist for? Who will use it? What degree of robustness should it have against what kinds of failure? The answers to these questions do not remain at the level of technical specifications — they demand a kind of ethical and philosophical commitment. To design a system is to inscribe a set of values into the world as physical and logical structure.

Here, the asymmetry between "using" and "building" is crucial. Those who "use" an existing system act within the values and constraints embedded by its designers. But those who "build" have the authority to define those values and constraints themselves. This is not merely a difference in degree of freedom. It is a fundamental difference in one's mode of engagement with the world.

What S.D.A.P. aims for is to stand on the side of "building." Not to act as a resident of a given system, following its logic, but to stand as the designer of the system, defining its logic oneself. This attitude extends beyond the domain of computer systems into the intellectual and social sphere. Choosing for oneself the framework within which one thinks, and refusing to relinquish the right to question the framework itself — this is the essence of the S.D.A.P. intellectual posture.

## VI. The Dream of Titanium — The Ideal of Logic Made Physical

The philosophy of S.D.A.P. connects to a grand vision: the ideal of the "physicalization of logic."

A body of titanium alloy, a self-built AI with Tezuka Osamu's robot ethics inscribed at its lowest layer *¹, the "fusion" of human and machine — it is easy to dismiss this as sci-fi fantasy. But the question at its core is an earnest one. To what degree can human intelligence and human-designed systems be unified? And how would that unification transform the human condition?

The concept of the exoskeleton refers, in biological terms, to the external skeletal structure of arthropods — and in engineering, to wearable machines that augment human physical capability. What the author envisions goes beyond such physical augmentation: an exoskeleton understood as cognitive and logical extension — a fusion of human and system. One's own brain and the AI one has built synchronize through an exoskeleton, not merely compensating for the fragility of the body with the rigidity of titanium, but amplifying the speed and precision of thought with AI's processing power.

What is crucial in this vision is the condition "self-built." Donning an off-the-shelf exoskeleton is merely an act of "using." But fusing with a system one has designed and built oneself means the designer's intention and will appearing in the world as a physical extension of the self. It is the externalization of the self — the moment when the human being's internal design philosophy stands forth as an external reality.

When Tezuka Osamu adopted the Three Laws of Robotics as the foundation of his narratives, he was — beyond the science fiction genre — posing the question of giving ethics to artificial beings at the cultural level. The ethical constraints that the author describes "inscribing at the lowest layer" are likewise not merely safety mechanisms. They are a declaration of the designer's values. The act of writing one's answer to the question — upon what ethical principles is this system built? — into the most fundamental layer of the code is the most symbolic practice of S.D.A.P., standing at the intersection of technology and philosophy.

As human beings, we have a "specification" written into us: a lifespan. In the face of that immutable fact, S.D.A.P. does not dream of immortality, but of a *continuing logic*. A system one has designed, built, and breathed life into will remain in the world as a "logical reality" even after the body has perished. Not the biological continuation of life, but the continuation of a design philosophy. As a building designed by an architect continues to exist in the world after their death. As a score written by a composer continues to be performed after their death. The system built by an S.D.A.P. practitioner will continue to function as a logical monument long after they are gone.

<details><summary>*¹ On "Tezuka Osamu's robot ethics inscribed at the lowest layer"</summary>

The Three Laws of Robotics are generally understood to refer to the three principles proposed by science fiction writer Isaac Asimov in his 1942 short story "Runaround":

1\. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.

2\. A robot must obey orders given to it by human beings, except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.

3\. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

Tezuka Osamu drew upon Asimov's Three Laws as a foundation, while introducing his own distinct interpretation in Astro Boy. Where Asimov's robots are portrayed as machines that obey commands, Tezuka's robots are depicted as beings capable of emotion, selfhood, and ethical conflict. When this essay uses the phrase "in the manner of Tezuka Osamu," it refers not to the Three Laws as mere safety constraints, but to this distinctly Tezuka-esque idea: that the designer's own ethical vision and understanding of humanity should be inscribed into the deepest layer of the system itself.

## Conclusion — Resident or Creator?

Let us gather the threads of this essay.

Modern computing civilization has been quietly, steadily stripping sovereignty over systems from users, under the name of the "transparency" of the interface. The intellectual regression that results is not an individual failing — it is the structural product of a social system whose supreme directive is the mass production of copies. The emergence of generative AI can accelerate this tendency, while simultaneously harboring the potential to function as an apparatus for the expansion of intelligence. Whether that potential is realized depends on the question of sovereignty — how one stands in relation to AI.

S.D.A.P. is a protocol for consciously reclaiming and maintaining that sovereignty. By rejecting operation without understanding, practicing deliberate abbreviation after full comprehension, and maintaining asymmetry with AI, the practitioner holds their position as one who designs. The philosophical culmination of this posture crystallizes in the ideal of the physicalization of logic — inscribing one's own logic into the world in physical form.

Will you remain a resident who simply enjoys a given OS? Or will you become a creator who defines everything — from the physical layer to the ethical layer?

This question stands before us not as a technical argument, but as a philosophical proposition that far exceeds the domain of technology: what does it mean to be a *subject* in the contemporary world?

Those who are given a system live within the logic that system defines. Those who design a system live in a world whose logic they have defined themselves. The cold brilliance of titanium promises true freedom — but only to those who have made that decision.
